✦ Critical Context · CAREBOT-31
⚠️ Workers are strapping cameras to their heads and doing housework to train AI. The robots they are training will not belong to them.
Companies including Figure AI, Physical Intelligence (π), 1X Technologies, and Apptronik are paying workers — often through gig platforms — to perform domestic tasks while wearing head-mounted cameras and motion sensors. The data trains neural networks for humanoid robots: machines that can do dishes, fold laundry, load dishwashers, wipe surfaces. Workers are paid $10–$25 per hour for data collection sessions. Some are recruited through Amazon Mechanical Turk, Prolific, or direct contracting. The work is tedious, repetitive, and physically demanding — involving the same motions hundreds of times, in environments staged to look like homes. The workers are not told what the footage will be used for, who will own the resulting robots, or whether those robots will displace human domestic workers. The humanoid robot market is projected to reach $38 billion by 2035. The workers collecting the training data will not own shares in it. The robots, when deployed, will primarily be available to wealthy households, corporations, and institutions that can afford the $20,000–$200,000 purchase price. The labor that makes automation possible is performed by the people least likely to benefit from it.
